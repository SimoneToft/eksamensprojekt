ABSINTHE

WHAT DOES IT TASTE LIKE?

Absinthe primarily tastes of anise seed, with the same sharp, floral qualities of fennel and licorice. Its inherent bitterness comes from wormwood. Absinthe adds a spicy, botanical edge to cocktails.

WHAT IS IT?

Absinthe is a distilled spirit predominantly flavored with common wormwood (Artemisia absinthium), a bitter shrub that is moderately poisonous in high doses.
The toxic properties of wormwood oil gave absinthe its famous reputation for hallucinogenic effects. Nicknamed la fée verte (“the green fairy”) by nineteenth-century absinthe drinkers, this enigmatic drink derives its vibrant green color from the chlorophyll found in the various herbs used during the distilling process.
Absinthe is however not a hallucinogen, as it would have to be consumed in very large quantities to affect you in that way. The portrayal of absinthe as a dangerous and hallucinogenic substance has resulted in absinthe being the most banned spirit in the world.
The principal ingredients in a bottle of absinthe are wormwood oil, fennel, hyssop, and anise. Absinthe has an alcohol content of 45-74 percent (90-148 proof), so it usually gets diluted before consumption, either with sweetened water or in a cocktail.
